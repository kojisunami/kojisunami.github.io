Interest

Algebraic Geometry
  • I am interested in sheaf/category theory. For example, étale cohomology solved Weil conjecture and it defined étale homotopy group, that corresponds to Galois theory. So number theory and algebraic topology might be the algebraic geometric problems, and the development of algebraic geometry is still an ongoing problem today.
  • I am interested in Mori's minimal model program with toric variety and log geometry.
  • I am interested in non-commutative perspective for algebraic geometry (e.g. differential graded algebra, field with one-element, D-module, perverse sheaves, derived algebraic geometry etc). For example, they give us alternative proofs of Weil conjecture from Deligne's original proof with Weil cohomology. They also study Langlands program, K-theory and Picard sheaves etc.
Mirror Symmetry
  • Mirror symmetry is a correspondence of the Frobenius manifold of A-model and B-model by Barannikov morphism, where A-model studies Gromov-Witten invariant, which is for quantum instaton, and B-model studies Period integral, which is for superconductivity. We care Frobenius manifold because it is studied for quantum group and Stokes matrix etc. One of my current interests in mirror symmetry is geometric Langlands program representing Gauge theory N=4 and its connection to SYZ conjecture and Higgs bundle through the language of derived algebraic geometry.
Lie Algebra
  • I am interested in geometric Satake correspondence, which is the correspondence of a category of D-module of Hecke categories and a category of representation of an algebraic group, which is partially proved by loop group and perverse sheaves. The application of geometric Satake correspondence is geometric Langlands program and quantum Satake correspondecen e.g. representation of quadric and Spinor variety by quantum cohomology.
  • Another interest in Lie Algebra is Okounkov's work, bridging statistical mechanics(e.g. random partition function), infinite-dimensional Lie algebra, and algebraic geometry(e.g. Calabi-Yau manifold), for A-model in mirror symmetry and crystal melting etc.
Geometry & Analysis & PDE & The Other

  • Satake Isomorphism (Dec 3 2022)
    Classical Satake isomorphism claims the spherical Hecke algebra (or double coset of algebraic group with p-adic number) has a correspondece with a representation ring of the algebraic group, and it was first formulated to prove Kazhdan-Lustzig formula. Geometric Satake isomorphism is its generalization, where naïvely we choose t-adic number in place of p-adic number to describe geometry, or less naïvely, correspondence of category of D-module of Hecke categories and category of representation of the algebraic group. Hecke category signifies open disk, which is why we call it "geometric". Geometric Satake isomorphism is used to study SYZ conjecture, homological mirror symmetry conjecture, Higgs bundle etc. Quantum Satake isomorphism relates representation of algebraic group to quantum cohomology(e.g. cohomology of Schubert cell) to study representation of spinor variety and Quadratic etc. This is for the talk in KU/KSU talk and Lie algebra(math814) final presentation.

  • Number theory with a view toward algebraic geometry (Oct 10 2022)
    There is no solution of quitic polynomial by roots of unity, namely, finite nonabelian Galois extension is not solvable, but it's solvable by infinite abelian Galois extension. Consider that any polynomial of rational coefficient has a factorization over finite field, the polynomial can be further factorized to the p-adic field(Hensel's lemma). Note that p-adic field is an infinite separable abelian extension of finite field, and this connects to Galois representation, so modular form and Elliptic curve, Shimura-Taniyama conjecture, Weil conjecture: this is the foundational problem toward defining scheme theory. In a more modern language, Grothendieck defined étale topology in scheme theory with a henselization of Zariski topology, and this helped to solve Weil conjecture by Deligne. This is for the talk for Graduate Student Seminar.
